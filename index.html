This format resembles an hourglass shape structure. In other words, it begins broadly, then focuses narrowly, and then broadens again. The introduction starts wide and then narrows down. For instance, it may first note the general importance of the subject, then summarise previous research on it, and then identify the specific question that the current research addressed. The methods section and results section focus narrowly on the research being reported. The discussion broadens out again. For instance, it may interpret the current findings, then relate them to other research, and then discuss their wider relevance.

2.1 Abstract

Written by Sanika Humbe

The abstract of the report aims to provide a summary of the purpose, main findings, and implications of the study. It gives a brief overview of the entire report, giving readers an idea of what to expect. It provides a statement of what the thesis will ask or explore rather than what it found. And it provides general categories rather than specific details in the findings In constructing the abstract, it is important to also include a synopsis of the thesis report, highlighting the background information related to the topic and emphasising the importance of the current study. Additionally, identifying gaps in previous research helps to establish the context for the study.

2.2 Introduction using CARS Model (Create a Research Space)

Written by Aadarsh Sasi Kumar

The introduction is the most important part of any scientific topic or paper because it helps the readers to understand the particular paper without reading the whole context. So basically by following the CARS model we can write a good introduction that describes the papers well.

As shown in (Figure 3), The model follows like " “Establishing the territory” the author tries to give a basic introduction about the topic. In the second one, “Establishing the niche” the author finds a gap in a certain topic which he or she takes as an initial motivation and tries to resolve through the research. The last one, “Occupying the niche”, is an important part of the CARS model, where the author tries to submit all the findings that he or she has done [5]."

Move 1 : Establishing the territory

"In this move, the author sets the context for his or her research, providing the necessary background on the topic [5]." There are three steps to satisfy the move.

Step 1 : Claiming centrality

As readers are also the writers they will be able to understand the domain which they are looking for by mentioning the centrality of the niche, so it's easy for them to categorise their research in a certain way. So as a writer it's important to mention under what domain the paper will fit in.

Examples:

" Recently there has been a spate of interest in ... [5] ".

Step 2 : Making topic generalisation

During the research process or in some time due to the problems they found in certain areas, writers write papers by highlighting that particular problem which will normally convert into the topic.

Examples:

" Properties of X are still not completely understood [5] " .

Step 3 : Reviewing Previous Items of Research

As this is a common step in almost every scientific paper, to find out to what extent that domain has been explored, we need previous research papers. By mentioning them the readers can widen their knowledge on the particular problem.

Examples:

" Both Johnson and Morgan claim that the biographical facts have been misrepresented [5] " .

Move 2 : Establishing the Niche

"In this move, the author argues that there is an open "niche" in the existing research, a space that needs to be filled through additional research. The author can establish a niche in one of the four ways [5] ".

So a beginner who is writing scientific papers, it's important to clearly describe our target audience and how we proceed with our findings. In a writer's point of view, giving a strong opinion on a topic is important, which adds value to the paper during the publishing.

Step 1A : Counter-claiming

As a writer as well as reader it will be helpful for them to make clear understanding of a particular topic which they are looking for , so by doing that they can clearly point out the previous research or an followed method is indicating something wrong about a certain topic.

Examples:

" While Jones and Riley believe X method to be accurate, a close examination demonstrates their method to be flawed [5] ".

Step 1B : Indicating a Gap

So in this particular move, we came to understand that the writer describes the gap he or she found during the research, but not mentioning how to tackle the problem, which will be followed in the later section.

Examples:

" While existing studies have clearly established X, they have not addressed Y [5] ".

Step 1C : Question-raising

The main difference between Step 1A and Step 1C is that Step 1A describes the problem which should be rectified, but Step 1C explains the unclear situation about a certain niche.

Examples:

" While Jones and Morgan have established X, these findings raise a number of questions, including ... [5] ".

Step 1D : Continuing a tradition

"The paper tries to follow the same pattern as the rest of the paper with an improved result at the end. [5]". So most of the papers in a scientific section we are able to see following this tradition.

Examples:

" Earlier studies seemed to suggest X. To verify this finding, more work is urgently needed [5] ".

Move 3 : Occupying the niche

The main difference between move 2 and move 3 is that the author shows some proof which he or she found after the research as an end result.

This is a crucial part of structuring a paper's introduction, because in move 2, we are providing our concrete view to write a paper. But through move 3 we confirm the readers that our claims are true, by giving the proof through our research.

There are four ways to occupy a niche according to CARs model,

Step lA : Outlining Purposes

" In this section the author provides the theme of the paper which he or she found, but not exactly the result of the paper [5]".

Examples:

" In this article I argue ... [5] ".

Step lB : Announcing present research

"The author describes the research in the current article [5]"

Examples:

" This paper describes three separate studies conducted between March 2008 and January 2009. [5] ".

Step 2 : Announcing principle findings

This section will come under the scope in some papers as in this section the author will mention to what extent the paper will cover, so the readers will get an early preview of the paper.

Examples:

" The results of the study suggest ... [5] ".

Step 3 : Indicating RA structure

" This section will come under the scope in some papers as in this section the author will mention to what extent the paper will cover [6] ", so the readers will get an early preview of the paper.

Examples:

" This paper is structured as follows ... [5] ".

2.3 Literature review

Written by Sanika Humbe

The literature review section serves to provide background information on the thesis topic. Reading papers related to background information, listing down some related sources, trying to understand what is missing from their research, all this critical discussion can be put in literature review. It answers the state of the field and aims to define the current understanding of the topic. To construct the literature review, it is necessary to conduct background research on the research question, evaluate and annotate sources, and look for patterns and gaps in the existing literature. By analysing where researchers agree and disagree in the field, a thesis literature review can be developed.

2.4 Methods

Written by Sanika Humbe

The methods section answers the question of what was done in the study and serves to describe the procedures step by step. Method is where you will include the experiment you are doing, your analysis, your inclusion criteria, your exclusion criteria and details on the Research Question. It is essential to explain why specific methods were chosen and to justify the statistical analyses conducted. This section also provides a clear understanding of the study's methodology.

2.5 Results

Written by Sanika Humbe

Result is the most important section of a thesis, which helps to show what it is that you've actually found. This is your contribution to the field. You can decide the results you want to present and the order in which you want to present them. So that some of the results get foregrounded while others get backgrounded. Make use of visuals, such as tables, figures, diagrams, charts to tell the particular story that you want to tell. The results section answers the question of what was found in the study. It describes the outcomes of the research and presents the data and facts discovered during the study. Key findings are outlined, providing a comprehensive overview of the study's results.

2.6 Discussion

Written by Sanika Humbe

Main body of the thesis includes the Method and Result section which are mostly descriptive but the discussion section will be more analytical and critical. Discuss and interpret your findings and connect them to previous research on the topic that is of relevance. And then broadens out to discuss the significance of these results to the field. It outlines the strengths and limitations of the study and highlights its relevance to current literature. The implications of the study are discussed. It also suggests future research.

Just like there are some moves in the introduction, a pattern of moves can also be determined in the case of the discussion. John Swales and Christine Feak have mapped these into the following five stages.

Move 1: Presentation of background information, it appears in some, but not all reports
Move 2 : Is the obligatory, which is summarising key results
Move 3 : Actual discussion of results takes place
Move 4 : Sometimes it might be necessary to mention the limitations of your study,And finally
Move 5 : Suggests further research or broader implications

2.7 Conclusion

Written by Sanika Humbe

The Conclusion is about how you want to conclude the thesis. It includes takeaway messages and what are the limitations along with what you've found. It summarises the overall findings of the study and their implications. It states the importance of the thesis statement and gives the thesis a sense of completeness. The conclusion leaves a final impression on the reader, highlighting the impact of the study.

2.8 References

Written by Sanika Humbe

The references section lists the literature that has made significant contributions to the field. It includes accurate citations, focusing on the most relevant, rigorous, and recent literature. Only published journal articles or books should be quoted in the references section.
